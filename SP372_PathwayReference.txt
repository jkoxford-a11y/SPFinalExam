PATHWAY 1: From Photon to Person — The Full Perceptual Stack
Sources: Ch. 1, 3, 4, 5, 6  ·  PPT 1, 3, 4, 5, 6
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

[1] Transduction
DEFINITION: The conversion of physical stimulus energy (light, sound, pressure) into electrochemical neural signals by specialized receptor cells.
EXAM PROMPT: What type of energy is converted, where, and what is produced? Why does the specific transduction mechanism limit what we can perceive?

[2] Center-surround RFs
DEFINITION: Retinal ganglion cell receptive fields with an excitatory center and inhibitory surround (or vice versa), created by lateral inhibition. Respond maximally to small spots at the center, minimally to diffuse light.
EXAM PROMPT: What happens to firing rate for (a) center only, (b) surround only, (c) both equally illuminated? Why does this make the system prefer contrast over uniform illumination?

[3] Lateral inhibition
DEFINITION: Inhibitory signals transmitted horizontally across the retina by horizontal and amacrine cells, creating center-surround antagonism and enhancing edge contrast.
EXAM PROMPT: What does lateral inhibition do to the neural signal at a border? Name one perceptual consequence visible in everyday scenes.

[4] Feature detectors
DEFINITION: Neurons in V1 (simple, complex, end-stopped cells) that respond selectively to specific stimulus properties: orientation, direction of motion, length. Discovered by Hubel & Wiesel.
EXAM PROMPT: How do simple and complex cells differ? What is the functional payoff of having both?

[5] Columnar organization
DEFINITION: Neurons with similar functional properties are arranged in vertical columns perpendicular to the cortical surface. Orientation columns in V1 were the first to be characterized.
EXAM PROMPT: What does columnar organization tell us about how the cortex represents information? How does this relate to localist vs. distributed coding?

[6] What / Where streams
DEFINITION: Ventral stream (V1 → IT → temporal lobe): object identity. Dorsal stream (V1 → parietal lobe): spatial location and action guidance. Double dissociation proven by patients D.F. and similar cases.
EXAM PROMPT: What is the evidence that these are truly separate systems? Describe a patient whose behavior proves the dissociation.

[7] LOC → FFA → PPA
DEFINITION: Lateral occipital complex (objects), Fusiform face area (faces, expertise), Parahippocampal place area (scenes, spatial layout). Specialized regions along the ventral stream.
EXAM PROMPT: What does the existence of these specialized regions tell us about modular vs. distributed representation? What is the debate?

[8] Top-down modulation
DEFINITION: Higher cortical areas send feedback signals downward, allowing prior knowledge, expectations, and attention to shape what is perceived at earlier processing stages.
EXAM PROMPT: Give an example of top-down modulation changing what you actually perceive (not just your response). How does this connect to predictive coding?

[9] Feature Integration Theory
DEFINITION: Treisman's model: features (color, shape, motion) are registered automatically in parallel in a preattentive stage; binding requires focused attention on a specific location.
EXAM PROMPT: What are illusory conjunctions and why do they occur? What do they tell us about the role of attention in perception?

[10] Inattentional blindness
DEFINITION: Failure to perceive a clearly visible but unexpected stimulus when attention is fully engaged with another task. Gorilla experiment (Simons & Chabris, 1999).
EXAM PROMPT: Where in the perceptual stack does the signal fail? How is this different from the signal never arriving at the retina?

PATHWAY 2: What the Brain Represents — From Edges to Abstract Concepts
Sources: Ch. 3, 4, 5  ·  PPT 3, 4, 5  ·  Roy (2017)  ·  Mellet et al. (2019)
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

[1] IT cortex
DEFINITION: Inferotemporal cortex: the apex of the ventral stream, containing neurons that respond to whole objects with large, position-tolerant receptive fields. Responds to complex shapes, faces, and bodies.
EXAM PROMPT: How does what IT neurons respond to differ from V1 neurons? What computational problem does that difference solve?

[2] Concept cells
DEFINITION: Single neurons in the human MTL (Quiroga et al.) that respond to a specific person or concept across all sensory modalities — photograph, written name, spoken name — but not to other stimuli.
EXAM PROMPT: What specifically distinguishes a concept cell from a simple feature detector? What does modality invariance tell us about how the brain stores meaning?

[3] Modality-invariant neurons
DEFINITION: Neurons that respond to the same concept regardless of the sensory format (visual, auditory, textual). Sydney Opera House neuron: responded to photographs from multiple angles, written name, and spoken name.
EXAM PROMPT: Give a concrete example from Roy (2017). What level of the hierarchy do these neurons occupy?

[4] Localist vs. distributed
DEFINITION: Localist: concepts represented by dedicated single neurons or small dedicated ensembles. Distributed: concepts encoded as patterns across large populations. Concept cell evidence supports localist but does not settle the debate.
EXAM PROMPT: What would pure localist coding predict about the effects of brain damage? Why does the concept cell evidence not fully settle the debate?

[5] Fusiform face area
DEFINITION: Region in the fusiform gyrus showing preferential response to faces (Kanwisher, 1997). Damage causes prosopagnosia. Expertise hypothesis (Gauthier): may be a general expert-recognition area trained on faces.
EXAM PROMPT: What is the strongest evidence for face specialization? What is the strongest evidence against it?

[6] fMRI / BOLD decoding
DEFINITION: fMRI measures blood oxygen level-dependent (BOLD) signal as a proxy for neural activity. Multivoxel pattern analysis (MVPA) allows decoding of perceptual content from activation patterns.
EXAM PROMPT: What exactly is being measured by the BOLD signal? What are the key limits of fMRI for drawing conclusions about single neurons?

PATHWAY 3: The Prediction Engine — The Brain Constructs Reality
Sources: Ch. 1, 5  ·  PPT 1, 5, 6
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

[1] Inverse projection problem
DEFINITION: Any retinal image is consistent with infinitely many possible distal stimuli. The retinal image alone is insufficient to determine what caused it — the brain must use prior knowledge to resolve the ambiguity.
EXAM PROMPT: State the problem in one sentence. Why can't the brain solve it using retinal information alone?

[2] Gestalt principles
DEFINITION: Perceptual grouping rules (proximity, similarity, good continuation, common fate, Prägnanz) that reflect internalized statistical regularities of natural environments — the physics of how objects behave.
EXAM PROMPT: Pick any two principles and explain why they work — not just what they are, but why the visual system uses them.

[3] Unconscious inference
DEFINITION: Helmholtz's likelihood principle: the visual system unconsciously infers the most probable distal cause of a proximal stimulus. Perception is a form of hypothesis testing, not passive reception.
EXAM PROMPT: Give a specific example of an unconscious inference you make every time you look at a scene. How does this relate to the inverse projection problem?

[4] Bayesian inference
DEFINITION: Perception = prior probability × likelihood. The brain combines knowledge about what is probable in the environment (prior) with incoming sensory evidence (likelihood) to reach the most probable interpretation.
EXAM PROMPT: How does the same ambiguous evidence lead to different conclusions depending on context? What is doing the work — the prior or the likelihood?

[5] Predictive coding
DEFINITION: Neural implementation of Bayesian inference: predictions flow downward from higher cortical levels; only the prediction error (mismatch) propagates upward to update representations. The brain sends expectations, not copies.
EXAM PROMPT: How is predictive coding mechanistically different from Helmholtz's likelihood principle? What does it add that Bayes alone cannot explain?

[6] Scene schema
DEFINITION: Stored knowledge about the typical contents and spatial layout of familiar environments (kitchens, offices, streets). Establishes strong priors that shape what is perceived when ambiguous input arrives.
EXAM PROMPT: How did Palmer (1975) demonstrate the scene schema? Connect this to predictive coding using the word "prior."

[7] Change blindness
DEFINITION: Failure to detect a large change in a visual scene when the change is concurrent with a brief disruption (flicker, cut, saccade) that removes the transient onset signal.
EXAM PROMPT: Why doesn't a large physical change always generate a prediction error signal? What is the critical role of the transient?

PATHWAY 4: Why Perception Is Ancient — Evolution, Ecology & the Adapted Brain
Sources: Ch. 3, 4, 5  ·  PPT 3, 4, 5  ·  Mellet et al. (2019)  ·  JNP (2008)
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

[1] Ecological RFs
DEFINITION: Receptive field properties across species reflect the statistical structure of their visual environment. Center-surround organization exploits the fact that natural scenes have high spatial correlations — nearby regions tend to be similar.
EXAM PROMPT: Why do center-surround RFs make ecological sense? What would the visual system look like if it evolved in a completely different physical environment?

[2] Expertise hypothesis
DEFINITION: Gauthier et al.: the FFA may be a general expert-recognition area. After Greeble training (7 hours), FFA activation to Greebles equals face-level activation. Car and bird experts show elevated FFA for their domain.
EXAM PROMPT: What did the Greeble experiment show? What newborn data complicates a purely experiential account?

[3] Prosopagnosia
DEFINITION: Selective inability to recognize familiar faces following FFA damage, while other object recognition and facial feature description remain intact. Strongest evidence for face-specialized neural architecture.
EXAM PROMPT: What is intact and what is impaired? Why does this support a modular view — and why doesn't it fully prove one?

[4] Holistic face processing
DEFINITION: Faces are processed as unified wholes, not as collections of independent features. Face inversion dramatically impairs recognition (much more than inversion of other objects) because it disrupts configural processing.
EXAM PROMPT: Why are inverted faces so much harder to recognize than other inverted objects? What does this tell us about how faces are encoded?

[5] Mellet et al. (2019)
DEFINITION: fMRI study: modern humans viewing 540,000–30,000-year-old engravings activated bilateral ventral pathway in an object-like pattern, and additionally activated the visual word form area (left FUS4). Primary visual cortex did not differentiate intact from scrambled engravings.
EXAM PROMPT: What two neural findings were key? Why does the scrambled-control result specifically matter? What can the study NOT conclude about the engravers' intentions?

[6] Ventral pathway conservation
DEFINITION: Major functional subdivisions of the ventral visual pathway are preserved across primates (monkeys and humans). Evolution has not profoundly modified its structure, supporting the application of modern-brain fMRI results to archaic hominins.
EXAM PROMPT: Why do Mellet et al. argue their results apply to archaic hominins? State the strongest version of the critic's objection.

SEVEN MASTER CROSSING POINTS
These are where exam questions live — where pathways intersect.
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

[1] Lateral inhibition × prediction error
Pathways involved: P1 · P2 · P3
Why it matters: Hardware wiring (P1) that shapes what the system represents (P2) and amplifies difference signals a prediction-error system depends on (P3). What is each level contributing — and how far back does the logic of prediction error go?

[2] FFA × expertise × evolution
Pathways involved: P1 · P2 · P4
Why it matters: Node in ventral stream (P1); modular vs. distributed debate (P2); innate prewiring vs. expertise, developmental trajectory (P4). The same brain region is evidence for three different theories simultaneously.

[3] Predictive coding
Pathways involved: P1 · P2 · P3 · P4
Why it matters: The most integrative concept in the course. Explains thresholds (P1), why the brain holds internal models (P2), why Gestalt works (P3), and why predictions are ecologically tuned to the environment (P4).

[4] Inattentional blindness × change blindness
Pathways involved: P1 · P3
Why it matters: Attentional gate failure (P1) AND stable prediction with no error signal generated (P3). Same behavioral outcome, two different mechanisms. Predictive coding unifies them partially — but fails to explain why large changes are missed in change blindness.

[5] Hominin engravings (Mellet 2019)
Pathways involved: P2 · P4 + methodology
Why it matters: fMRI methodology; ventral pathway processes marks as objects (P2); 500,000-year roots of symbolic visual cognition (P4); Hodgson hypothesis refuted by the scrambled-control result.

[6] Weber's Law × predictive coding
Pathways involved: P1 · P3
Why it matters: Psychophysics methodology (P1); reframed as measuring sensitivity of a prediction-error system (P3). The JND being proportional to background intensity is exactly what a system encoding deviations from expectation would predict.

[7] Columnar organization × representation
Pathways involved: P1 · P2 · P4
Why it matters: Architectural principle of cortex (P1); basis for population coding and localist representation debate (P2); conserved across species in visual, somatosensory, and auditory cortex (P4).
