Lateral inhibition / center-surround RFs — crosses P1, P2, P3. It is hardware (the wiring of ganglion cells), representation (it explains why neurons respond to edges rather than diffuse light), and prediction (center-surround antagonism amplifies difference signals, which is exactly what a prediction-error system needs). A question here can reach from retinal anatomy all the way to Bayesian inference.
FFA & face perception — crosses P1, P2, P4. Structurally it is a node in the ventral what-pathway (P1). Representationally it is the debate between modular and distributed coding (P2). Evolutionarily it raises the expertise hypothesis and the question of whether face specialization is innate or learned (P4). Prosopagnosia is a clean clinical anchor.
Inattentional & change blindness — crosses P1, P3. These are failures of the attentional gate (P1 top of stack). But they are equally explained by predictive coding: if the brain already has a stable prediction of the scene, it generates no error signal for changes it does not attend to (P3). This is one of the richest exam questions in the whole course.
Thresholds, Weber's Law & psychophysics — crosses P1, P3. Absolute threshold = the signal level needed to cross from bottom-up input to conscious perception. Difference threshold (Weber) = the signal level needed to generate a prediction-error signal large enough to notice. Psychophysics is thus the measurement methodology that lives at the intersection of hardware and prediction.
Self-reference hook
You were so absorbed in your phone that you missed someone calling your name. Walk that failure from the photoreceptor all the way up — where exactly did the signal die, and what was the brain doing instead?

FFA: modular vs. distributed debate — the same structure crosses P1 (ventral stream), P2 (what does a brain area represent?), and P4 (is it innate or learned?). The Greeble expertise experiment and the Kanwisher face-selectivity studies represent two answers to the same question: does the brain have dedicated modules, or does specialization emerge from experience?
fMRI decoding & MVPA — Kamitani & Tong (orientation decoding) and Nishimoto (movie reconstruction) are simultaneously a methodology lesson (how fMRI voxel patterns encode information) and a representation lesson (what does it mean that a decoder can read the brain like a dictionary?). This question bridges P2 and P3 — if the brain holds decodable representations, it holds something like internal models.
Hominin engravings (Mellet et al. 2019) — the capstone for P2 and P4 together. The engravings activate the ventral pathway and the visual word form area — the same regions activated by objects and written symbols. This means the neural architecture for symbolic processing is at least 500,000 years old. The question for students: does this mean early hominins intended symbolic meaning, or that modern brains project meaning onto patterns that the visual system is already tuned to detect?
Self-reference hook
A song comes on and you instantly see a specific place. A smell brings back a face. Map that experience onto the hierarchy of representation we studied — from the single neuron that fires to the spoken name and the photograph, all the way to the abstract concept.

Predictive coding

Weber / thresholds as prediction errors
Predictive coding — the single most integrative concept in the course. It explains Gestalt laws (the brain learned environmental regularities and uses them as priors), thresholds (a stimulus must exceed the prediction to generate an error signal), inattentional blindness (no error = no update = no notice), and the Rat-Man demonstration (prior context sets the prediction that disambiguates the percept). A question asking students to explain any perceptual phenomenon through predictive coding forces them to synthesize almost the whole course.
Gestalt laws as ecological regularities — crosses P3 and P4. The laws of proximity, similarity, good continuation, and common fate are not arbitrary — they reflect statistical regularities in natural scenes. This makes them simultaneously prediction mechanisms (P3) and adaptations (P4). The visual system learned these regularities because they are useful for survival, not because they are logically necessary.
Weber's Law / thresholds as prediction errors — crosses P1, P3. An absolute threshold is the signal level at which bottom-up input is strong enough to override the brain's current prediction. A difference threshold (Weber) is how much change is needed to generate a prediction-error signal. This reframes classical psychophysics entirely — Fechner was measuring the sensitivity of a prediction-error system, not a passive sensor.
Self-reference hook
You walked into your kitchen this morning and "saw" it. How much of what you experienced was actually on your retina? Use predictive coding, scene schemas, and Gestalt regularities to describe what your brain actually did in the first 250 milliseconds.

FFA & face evolution — the newborn face-tracking data (Morton & Johnson) suggests the FFA connection is prewired, setting up a developmental trajectory from birth to adolescence. The expertise hypothesis asks whether this wiring is face-specific or domain-general. Both sides of the debate have ecological arguments: faces are the most important social stimuli (specificity view), or the system is a general-purpose expert-recognition module (expertise view).
Gestalt laws as adaptations (P3 × P4) — physical regularities in natural scenes (surfaces tend to be uniform, objects tend to move together, contours tend to be smooth) are the statistical structure that the visual system learned to expect. Gestalt laws are the behavioral fingerprint of that learning. They are simultaneously prediction mechanisms and adaptations to the environment in which our ancestors evolved.
Multisensory integration as survival (P2 × P4) — defense-related neurons in premotor cortex respond to looming visual, auditory, and somatosensory signals. The neocortex is essentially multisensory (Ghazanfar & Schroeder 2006) because real-world threats arrive across modalities simultaneously. The Roy (2017) hierarchy of abstraction from bi-modal neurons to concept cells is not just a representational story — it is an ecological one: organisms that integrate modalities survive better.
Self-reference hook
Your visual system was shaped by hundreds of millions of years of evolution. Pick three specific design features we studied and argue that each one is an adaptation to a specific environmental challenge. Now explain why that same ancient system can process a 500,000-year-old abstract engraving.
